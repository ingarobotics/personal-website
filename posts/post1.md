---
title: The Sequencing Problem
date: 2026-06-07
---

My bar for “making it” as a species is when we can casually coordinate large-scale engineering - when moving the matter of our own planet around to build something better[^1] is a normal thing we do, not a moonshot. That single capability is enough, because reaching it would mean we’d quietly solved two much harder things underneath. First, coordination: you don’t run century-long projects without having figured out how to keep humans aligned across time. Second, energy.

Energy is the one that unlocks everything else. The moment it stops being scarce, a whole category of problems we’ve filed under “uneconomical” quietly reopens. Water desalination at scale, and the freedom to move that water anywhere - so fresh water stops being a question of weather and geography. Which means food shortages end too - with water and energy to spare, you can grow food in greenhouses in Antarctica. Transportation gets cheap enough that where you live stops being a function of where you work. And once moving physical matter is cheap, you get the fun stuff: floating cities, artificial islands, new coastlines - literally, world building.

Once energy’s solved, the only thing left to spend is time - how fast you can move and assemble all that matter. The way out is teleportation, or a matter compiler: assemble anything from anything, right where you need it, so nothing has to travel at all - straight out of The Diamond Age. But that’s a whole other essay.

Here’s the catch I keep circling back to. All of that runs on abundant energy, and we don’t have abundant energy yet. We have to bootstrap our way there, and bootstrapping has an order of operations.

Think about what it takes to build the first generation of abundant-energy infrastructure - let’s say a Dyson swarm fed by asteroid mining. It takes materials. Specific ones, in concentrated form. And the unsettling thing about concentrated reserves is that they are a one-time endowment. The rich ores, the easily-refined deposits - those aren't renewable on any human timescale. They're geological accidents, pockets where the right elements happened to pile up over hundreds of millions of years.

Now, you might think materials can't really be the binding constraint, because Earth is a closed system. Short of launching things into orbit, every atom we "use up" is still here. We don't destroy it; we just scatter it. And with abundant energy, scattered is fine — you can re-concentrate anything. Mine the ocean, mine the landfill, mine the tailings pile. Energy buys back dispersal.

But look closely at the dependency in that sentence. Re-concentrating dispersed matter is something you can afford after you have abundant energy. Before then, it's hopeless. And the materials you need to build the energy infrastructure in the first place have to come from the concentrated reserves - the ones you can still refine cheaply with the energy you have now.

So there's a trap, and it's shaped like a deadlock. If we spend the concentrated endowment on the wrong things — disposable electronics, billions of humanoids, products whose materials we never recover — before we've built the infrastructure, we end up stranded in the worst possible spot. We can't build the energy infrastructure, because the cheap inputs are gone. And we can't recover the scattered inputs, because that requires the very energy abundance we never reached. Both doors close at once.

That's why I've come to think of this less as a resource problem and more as a sequencing problem. The danger was never really "do we have enough material." We probably do. The danger is spending a non-renewable, one-time endowment in the wrong sequence. You won’t see it coming in any market - a real shortage spikes a price and someone reacts, but burning your one-time reserves out of turn moves nothing, right up until the options are gone. What decides the outcome isn’t how much you have, it’s the order you use it in.

To check whether we’re getting the order right, I’d ask: for any resource X, do we have enough X to build the infrastructure that gets us more X? You can ask it of neodymium, of gallium, of copper, one element at a time. But run the chain down far enough and every answer bottoms out at the same X. It's energy. Everything else is just the order in which we spend our one shot at getting there.

[^1]: My picture of "better" is, of all things, a yogurt commercial — Chobani's *Dear Alice*. Watch it once with sound on: nature and technology in perfect symbiosis, energy that's just *there*, local weather engineering, self-sufficiency living - everyone growing their own food and somehow still finding time for each other. Just about everything I'd want us to build, in one ad.
